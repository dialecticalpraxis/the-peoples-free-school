# Primary Sources

Documents, speeches, manifestos, archives, and oral histories that let the room encounter a period, a movement, or a thinker directly rather than through secondary description.

## How to use this index

Primary sources are valuable for the historical lens especially, but they appear across other lenses too. A manifesto is a primary source for the liberatory lens. A worker's testimony is a primary source for the experiential lens. A theorist's own words are primary sources for the theoretical lens.

## A note on reading primary sources

Primary sources can be harder to read than secondary scholarship. The language is from another time. The references assume context the reader does not have. The author is making a case rather than explaining themselves to outsiders.

This difficulty is part of the work. The room is encountering history as it sounded, not as a historian has digested it. Take time. Read aloud when it helps. Look up references together. Let the strangeness of the source teach what it has to teach.
