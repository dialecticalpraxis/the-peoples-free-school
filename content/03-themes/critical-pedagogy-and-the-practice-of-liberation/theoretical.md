_How do we understand and analyze this? What concepts and frameworks help us see what is happening?_

See [[02-pedagogy#Theoretical|the pedagogy document]] for what this lens does across all themes.

The core concepts this lens builds are the ones the school runs on: the banking model of education and its problem-posing alternative, conscientization (reading the word and the world together), dialogue, the organic intellectual, and the presupposition of equal intelligence. This is the theme where the school examines its own operating assumptions, which means reading Freire and his companions not as authorities to defer to, but as thinkers to argue with.

## Anchor questions

- What does Freire mean by the "banking model" of education? Have you been on the receiving end of it?
- What is "conscientization," reading the word and the world together?
- Why does Freire insist that no one teaches another, and no one learns alone?
- Is teaching ever neutral? What does bell hooks mean by teaching to transgress?
- Who are the "organic intellectuals," and can everyone be one?

## Materials

Free unless marked otherwise. A few of these are widely hosted copies of in-copyright books; that is noted where it applies.

### Foundational texts

- **Paulo Freire** (1921 to 1997), _Pedagogy of the Oppressed_ (1970). The founding text of critical pedagogy: the banking model, conscientization, and dialogue as the practice of liberation. Dense in places, and best read a chapter at a time with the group. Free PDF: [files.commons.gc.cuny.edu](https://s3.amazonaws.com/files.commons.gc.cuny.edu/wp-content/blogs.dir/1076/files/2012/10/Freire-Pedagogy-of-the-Oppressed.pdf)
- **bell hooks** (1952 to 2021), _Teaching to Transgress_ (1994). Extends Freire through feminism and Black experience, arguing for education as a practice of freedom that engages the whole person, including the teacher. Warmer and more personal than Freire, and a good companion to him. Widely hosted, though in copyright: [academictrap.wordpress.com](https://academictrap.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/bell-hooks-teaching-to-transgress.pdf)
- **Myles Horton** (1905 to 1990) and **Paulo Freire**, _We Make the Road by Walking_ (1990). Six days of recorded dialogue between the two founders, comparing Highlander's and Freire's methods and how liberatory education actually gets organized. The most practical of the theory. Widely hosted, though in copyright: [codkashacabka.wordpress.com](https://codkashacabka.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/07/we-make-the-road-by-walking-myles-and-paolo-freie-book.pdf)

### The intellectual, hegemony, and equality

- **Antonio Gramsci** (1891 to 1937), _The Intellectuals_ (from the Prison Notebooks). Introduces organic versus traditional intellectuals and the argument that education creates rather than reflects the political order. Central to how a movement school understands its own role, and shared with [[03-themes/the-state-strategy-and-power/index|The State, Strategy, and Power]]. Free: [marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/gramsci/prison_notebooks/problems/intellectuals.htm)
- **Jacques Rancière** (b. 1940), _The Ignorant Schoolmaster_ (1987). Through the story of Joseph Jacotot, Rancière argues from the presupposition of equal intelligence, a sharp challenge to any pedagogy, including a Freirean one, that positions the teacher as the one who knows. Free: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/TheIgnorantSchoolmaster-English)
- **Gabriel Rockhill**, _Who Paid the Pipers of Western Marxism?_ (2024). Turns Gramsci's question about the intellectual back onto the school's own reading list, arguing that Cold War institutions, including the CIA, helped shape "Western Marxism" into a safely academic, anti-communist-compatible form. A provocation that forces the room to ask who funded and housed the theorists it reads, and what that did to their politics. Not free; [publisher page](https://monthlyreview.org/9781685901349/).
- **Jacobin**, _No, Western Marxism Wasn't a CIA Plot_ (2026). A critical response arguing that Rockhill overstates the case and mistakes institutional entanglement for conspiracy. Read the two together: the point is not to accept or dismiss the thesis, but to practice on the school's own sources the reflexive suspicion that both Gramsci and Rockhill demand. Free: [jacobin.com](https://jacobin.com/2026/04/review-rockhill-western-marxism-cold-war)

### Contemporary and companion

- **Henry Giroux** (b. 1943), _Critical Pedagogy and the Postmodern/Modern Divide_ (2004). A concise statement of critical pedagogy as a project for democratic public life against neoliberal schooling. Free PDF: [files.eric.ed.gov](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ795233.pdf)
- **Antonia Darder** (b. 1952), _Reinventing Paulo Freire: A Pedagogy of Love_ (2002). Reworks Freire through the testimony of teachers and a pedagogy of love, guarding against turning his ideas into a fixed method or a slogan. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/reinventingpaulo0000dard_2ed)

## A note on method

There is an irony to studying critical pedagogy in a lecture, and the group should feel it. The safest way to betray Freire is to treat _Pedagogy of the Oppressed_ as a sacred text to be banked into the room. Read it dialogically, against the room's own experience of school, and against its critics in the liberatory lens. The theory is only worth anything if the way the group studies it already practices what it preaches.

This lens also asks the school to be honest about its own bookshelf. Gramsci insists that intellectuals are produced by particular institutions and classes; Rockhill presses the uncomfortable version of that question directly at the reading list of a project like this one: who paid, housed, and promoted the theorists we cite, and how did that shape what they could say? The answer is not to purge the canon but to read it with that suspicion switched on, and to notice that a school funded by no one and run by its participants is trying to stand somewhere other than the university seminar.
