_How did this come to be? What were the conditions, struggles, and contingencies that produced the current arrangement?_

See [[02-pedagogy#Historical|the pedagogy document]] for what this lens does across all themes.

Race is not old. As a way of sorting humanity into ranked, inheritable groups, it was made alongside capitalism, in the same centuries and often by the same hands. This lens traces that making: the Atlantic slave trade and the wealth it produced, the invention of whiteness as a tool to divide laboring people, the overthrow of Reconstruction, and the long machinery of Jim Crow, convict leasing, and redlining. It builds on [[03-themes/how-capitalism-works/historical|the historical lens of the capitalism theme]], which introduces the slavery and capitalism link.

## Anchor questions

- Race did not always exist as we know it. When and why was it invented, and by whom?
- How did slavery, and the wealth it produced, shape the world we live in now?
- How were poor whites and enslaved and free Black people kept from making common cause? Who arranged that?
- What happened to the brief experiment of Reconstruction, and who overturned it?
- How did the law build and rebuild the color line, from slave codes to Jim Crow to redlining?
- Whose resistance is left out of the story?

## Materials

Free unless marked otherwise. "Free to borrow" means a free Internet Archive account and a renewable one-hour loan.

### Books

- **Eric Williams** (1911 to 1981), _Capitalism and Slavery_ (1944). The foundational argument that Atlantic slavery and the slave trade financed Britain's industrial rise, and that abolition followed once slavery no longer served capital. Shared with [[03-themes/how-capitalism-works/historical|the capitalism theme]]. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/capitalismslaver00will_1)
- **Theodore W. Allen** (1919 to 2005), _The Invention of the White Race, Volume 1_ (1994). Argues that "the white race" was a deliberate ruling-class invention in colonial Virginia, a system of social control built after Bacon's Rebellion to keep laboring people divided. Free PDF: [ouleft.org](https://ouleft.org/wp-content/uploads/Allen-The-Invention-of-the-White-Race-vol-1.pdf)
- **Edmund S. Morgan** (1916 to 2013), _American Slavery, American Freedom_ (1975). Traces how colonial Virginia produced American ideals of freedom and racial slavery together, the paradox at the root of the racial order. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/americanslaverya00morg)
- **W.E.B. Du Bois** (1868 to 1963), _Black Reconstruction in America_ (1935). The landmark revision that centers the agency of formerly enslaved people, introduces the "general strike" of the enslaved, and names the "public and psychological wage" of whiteness. The single most important book for this theme. Free: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/w-e-b-du-bois-black-reconstruction-an-essay-toward-a-history-of-the-part-which-b_202304)
- **Douglas A. Blackmon**, _Slavery by Another Name_ (2008). Documents convict leasing, debt peonage, and the legal machinery that re-enslaved Black labor after emancipation. A direct link between Jim Crow and profit. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/slaverybyanother00blac_0)
- **Richard Rothstein**, _The Color of Law_ (2017). Shows that redlining and residential segregation were de jure government policy, a primary engine of the racial wealth gap that persists today. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/coloroflawforgot0000roth)
- **Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz** (b. 1938), _An Indigenous Peoples' History of the United States_ (2014). Recasts US history as settler colonialism and Indigenous dispossession, essential for situating racial capitalism alongside land and elimination. A bridge to [[03-themes/imperialism-and-the-global-system/index|Imperialism and the Global System]]. Free: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/an-indigenous-peoples-history-of-the-united-states-ortiz)

### Primary sources

- **Ida B. Wells** (1862 to 1931), _Southern Horrors: Lynch Law in All Its Phases_ (1892). Wells's investigative pamphlet exposing lynching as a tool of economic and political terror rather than a response to crime. Short, and a model of research turned into a weapon. Public domain and free: [gutenberg.org](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/14975)
- **Loving v. Virginia** (1967). Worth reading for Virginia's own arithmetic: "any ascertainable Negro blood" made you colored, but under one-sixteenth "American Indian" blood did not, so the First Families could keep their Pocahontas story. Racecraft, in statute. Free: [law.cornell.edu](https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/388/1)

### Films and documentaries

- **Slavery by Another Name** (Sam Pollard, 2012, 90 min). The PBS documentary of Blackmon's book, on how forced labor, convict leasing, and peonage re-enslaved African Americans from 1865 until World War II. Connects the historical record to its afterlives, and streams free. [pbs.org](https://www.pbs.org/show/slavery-another-name/)

### Video

- **"Slavery in the American Colonies: Crash Course Black American History #2"** (CrashCourse, Clint Smith, 2021). Roughly 11 minutes, about 758,000 views. Clint Smith on how chattel slavery became law and economic engine in the early colonies. A reliable building block for a historical session. [youtube.com/watch?v=G4syEkyOzmY](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=G4syEkyOzmY)
- **"Housing Segregation and Redlining in America: A Short History"** (NPR, Code Switch, 2018). Roughly 7 minutes, about 1.4 million views. How federal redlining and the unenforced Fair Housing Act built lasting segregation and wealth disparity. A clean visual companion to Rothstein. [youtube.com/watch?v=O5FBJyqfoLM](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O5FBJyqfoLM)

## Status

This lens has a developed first pass for this theme. The expansion of whiteness and the racialization of Asian and Latino people are now covered; Indigenous dispossession is deliberately held for a forthcoming theme on settler colonialism and land, which can carry it properly rather than as a subsection here. Contributions welcome, especially Filipino racialization and the colonial ambiguity of US nationals, and the global history of race beyond the United States.
