_What would it mean to overcome or transform this? What have movements imagined, attempted, and built?_

See [[02-pedagogy#Liberatory|the pedagogy document]] for what this lens does across all themes. The school does not resolve the disagreements among traditions. It puts them in front of the room and lets the room think.

The Black radical tradition is not one program but an argument that has run for two centuries. This lens gathers its poles: self-emancipation and anticolonial revolution, Black feminism and abolition, and the long disputes over nationalism versus coalition and over reparations. The central question is whether a world without racial domination is possible without ending capitalism, and the traditions here answer it differently on purpose.

## Anchor questions

- What would a world without racial domination require? Is it possible without ending capitalism?
- Abolition of what, exactly, and built in place of what?
- Integration, separation, or transformation: what have Black freedom traditions argued, and why do they disagree?
- What do reparations mean, and what would they have to repair?
- What have movements already built that shows another world is possible?

## Materials

Free unless marked otherwise. This lens deliberately gathers positions that disagree with each other.

### Self-emancipation and anticolonial liberation

- **C.L.R. James** (1901 to 1989), _The Black Jacobins_ (1938). The classic account of the Haitian Revolution as the only successful large-scale slave revolt, a founding text of the Black radical tradition and of the idea that the enslaved freed themselves. Free: [libcom.org](https://libcom.org/article/black-jacobins-toussaint-louverture-and-san-domingo-revolution-clr-james)
- **Frantz Fanon** (1925 to 1961), _The Wretched of the Earth_ (1961). The anticolonial analysis of decolonization, national consciousness, and violence, foundational for thinking about liberation on a world scale. A bridge to [[03-themes/imperialism-and-the-global-system/index|Imperialism and the Global System]]. Free: [libcom.org](https://libcom.org/article/wretched-earth-frantz-fanon)
- **Frantz Fanon**, _Black Skin, White Masks_ (1952). A psychological study of how colonialism and anti-Black racism deform selfhood, essential for the interior dimension of liberation that the anticolonial texts leave out. Free: [libcom.org](https://libcom.org/article/black-skin-white-masks-frantz-fanon)

### Black feminism and abolition

- **Combahee River Collective**, _The Combahee River Collective Statement_ (1977). The Black feminist statement that articulates interlocking oppressions and an integrated, socialist analysis. A key origin point for intersectional liberation politics. Free: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/Combahee1979)
- **Angela Y. Davis** (b. 1944), _Are Prisons Obsolete?_ (2003). A short, accessible case for prison abolition that traces the prison's roots in slavery and argues for decarceral alternatives. A strong first reading on abolition. No borrowable copy exists. Paywalled: [sevenstories.com](https://www.sevenstories.com/books/2907-are-prisons-obsolete), and see [[04-resources/index#A note on access|the note on access]] for how to get it.
- **Mariame Kaba**, _So You're Thinking About Becoming an Abolitionist_ (2020). A short primer on what prison-industrial-complex abolition means as vision and strategy, a good single reading to anchor a first session before the longer texts. Free PDF: [interruptingcriminalization.com](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5345d0c1e4b0b2e29e338463/t/603eb08af6bc5006a9506bda/1614721162714/So+Youre+Thinking+About+Becoming+an+Abolitionist.pdf)
- **Mariame Kaba**, _We Do This 'Til We Free Us_ (2021). The book behind the primer: essays and interviews treating abolition as everyday organizing practice rather than a slogan, drawn from decades of campaigns. Behind a paywall, and worth chasing down; see [[04-resources/index#A note on access|the note on access]]. Not free: [haymarketbooks.org](https://www.haymarketbooks.org/books/1664-we-do-this-til-we-free-us)

### Debates and disagreements

The school holds the disagreements open. These pairs stage two of the longest-running arguments in Black freedom politics.

- **Malcolm X** (1925 to 1965), _The Ballot or the Bullet_ (1964). The major statement of Black nationalism, self-determination, and self-defense, the nationalist pole of the strategy debate. Free (abridged transcript): [teachingamericanhistory.org](https://teachingamericanhistory.org/document/the-ballot-or-the-bullet-2/)
- **Bayard Rustin** (1912 to 1987), _From Protest to Politics_ (1965). A social-democratic argument for coalition politics and economic reform over separatism, set here deliberately against Malcolm X. Free: [crmvet.org](https://www.crmvet.org/docs/rustin65.pdf)
- **Ta-Nehisi Coates**, _The Case for Reparations_ (2014). The influential case that reparations follow from a documented history of plunder from slavery through redlining. The pro-reparations position. Free, resolves in a browser: [theatlantic.com](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2014/06/the-case-for-reparations/361631/)
- **Adolph Reed Jr.** (b. 1947), _The Case Against Reparations_ (2000). A left, class-first critique arguing that reparations politics is a poor strategy that sidesteps universal redistribution. The counter-position to Coates. Free: [nonsite.org](https://nonsite.org/the-case-against-reparations/)
- Malcolm X and Rustin disagree about whether Black freedom runs through separation or coalition. Coates and Reed disagree about whether justice runs through race-specific repair or universal redistribution. Put each pair side by side and let the room argue it rather than resolving it.

A lecturer-style walkthrough, best as facilitator preparation or a companion to the reading. [youtube.com/watch?v=DJmyNQN7fmM](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DJmyNQN7fmM)
