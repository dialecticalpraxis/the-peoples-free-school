_What would it mean to overcome or transform this? What have movements imagined, attempted, and built?_

See [[02-pedagogy#Liberatory|the pedagogy document]] for what this lens does across all themes. The school does not resolve the disagreements among traditions. It puts them in front of the room and lets the room think.

This is the oldest and sharpest argument on the left: what do you do about the state? Take it over through elections and reform. Smash it and build workers' councils in its place. Abolish it and refuse to build a new one. Work on its contradictions from inside while building movements outside. Each position has a century of experience and defeat behind it. This lens sets them side by side and lets the room weigh them.

## Anchor questions

- Smash the state or take it over? What does each side actually propose?
- Reform or revolution: can the state be voted into serving the people?
- If the state withers away, what replaces it, and who decides?
- Anarchists and Leninists both want a world without the boss. Why do they disagree so sharply about the state?
- What would self-government by ordinary people actually look like?

## Materials

Free unless marked otherwise. This lens deliberately gathers positions that disagree with each other. Read them as an argument, not a syllabus.

### Smash and replace the state

- **V.I. Lenin** (1870 to 1924), _The State and Revolution_ (1917). The strategic argument to smash the bourgeois state and replace it with the rule of the workers organized through soviets, and the fullest statement of the "withering away" thesis. The anchor of the revolutionary position. Free: [marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/lenin/works/1917/staterev/index.htm)
- **V.I. Lenin**, _The Dual Power_ (1917). A short article defining the situation where soviets and the official government coexist. The historical reference point for later "dual power" strategy. Free: [marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/lenin/works/1917/apr/09.htm)

### The parliamentary and reformist road

- **Eduard Bernstein** (1850 to 1932), _Evolutionary Socialism_ (1899). The founding statement of the reformist, parliamentary road, included as a genuine opposing position: that socialism can arrive gradually through democracy and reform. Free: [marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bernstein/works/1899/evsoc/)
- **Karl Kautsky** (1854 to 1938), _The Road to Power_ (1909). The orthodox strategy of building a mass party to win the state, a bridge and a foil between the revolutionary and reformist camps, and a live reference in today's electoral debates. Free: [marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/kautsky/1909/power/index.htm)

### Reform or revolution

- **Rosa Luxemburg** (1871 to 1919), _Reform or Revolution_ (1900). Her reply to Bernstein, insisting that reforms are a means and not a substitute for the conquest of power, and defending mass action. Shared with [[03-themes/how-capitalism-works/liberatory|the capitalism theme]]. Free: [marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/luxemburg/1900/reform-revolution/index.htm)

### The anarchist and council alternatives

- **Mikhail Bakunin** (1814 to 1876), _Marxism, Freedom and the State_ (1872). Extracts on the Bakunin-against-Marx quarrel, warning that a workers' state would breed a new ruling class of ex-workers and bureaucrats. The anarchist prophecy the room can weigh against the Soviet record. Free: [theanarchistlibrary.org](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/mikhail-bakunin-marxism-freedom-and-the-state)
- **Errico Malatesta** (1853 to 1932), _An Anarchist Programme_ (1920). A practical anarchist platform on aims and methods, rejecting the capture of state power in favor of abolishing it through direct organization. Free: [theanarchistlibrary.org](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/errico-malatesta-an-anarchist-programme)
- **Anton Pannekoek** (1873 to 1960), _Workers' Councils_ (1947). The council communist vision of workers governing production and society through their own councils rather than through party or parliament. Shared with [[03-themes/labor-work-and-organizing/liberatory|the labor theme]]. Free: [marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/pannekoe/1947/workers-councils.htm)

### The democratic road

- **Nicos Poulantzas** (1936 to 1979), _The State, Social Movements, Party_ (interview, 1979). His late case for a democratic road that works on the internal contradictions of the state while sustaining autonomous movements from below. The most influential contemporary attempt to get past the smash-versus-reclaim impasse. Free: [viewpointmag.com](https://viewpointmag.com/2017/12/18/state-social-movements-party-interview-nicos-poulantzas-1979/)

### Radical democracy and post-Marxism

- **Ernesto Laclau** (1935 to 2014) and **Chantal Mouffe** (b. 1943), _Hegemony and Socialist Strategy_ (1985). The founding post-Marxist argument that socialist strategy should give up the privileged role of the working class and build hegemony through a "chain of equivalence" linking many democratic struggles: feminist, ecological, anti-racist, and more. The most influential contemporary case for a broad radical-democratic politics rather than a class-centered one. Widely hosted, though in copyright: [files.libcom.org](https://files.libcom.org/files/ernesto-laclau-hegemony-and-socialist-strategy-towards-a-radical-democratic-politics.compressed.pdf)
- **Ellen Meiksins Wood** (1942 to 2016), _The Retreat from Class_ (1986). The classic Marxist rebuttal, arguing that Laclau and Mouffe's turn to discourse and equivalence dissolves class and cuts socialism loose from its material base. It won the Deutscher Prize and is the other half of the argument. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/retreatfromclass0000wood). Her short _Reply to Callinicos_ (1987) restates the case for free: [marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/wood/1987/xx/callinicos.html)

### Debates and disagreements

The school holds the disagreements open. This lens is built entirely around one of them.

- Lenin (smash the state), Bernstein and Kautsky (win it through parliament), Luxemburg (reform as means, not end), Bakunin and Malatesta (abolish it), Pannekoek (replace it with councils), Poulantzas (transform it from within and without), and Laclau and Mouffe (build a radical-democratic hegemony beyond class) do not agree, and Ellen Meiksins Wood insists that last move gives the game away. Set them against each other and ask which reading of history each one rests on. The practical lens brings this argument into the present through the debate over electoral versus movement strategy.
