_Lived experience as a source of knowledge. What has the room been through? What do participants already know from the inside?_

See [[02-pedagogy#Experiential|the pedagogy document]] for what this lens does across all themes.

Few themes are as likely to divide a room as this one. Someone's grandparents may have built a revolution; someone else's may have fled one. The same society that ran a literacy campaign also ran a labor camp. This lens refuses the two easy stories, the socialist paradise and the totalitarian nightmare, and starts instead from testimony that holds the contradictions together. The point is not to reach a verdict but to hear what living through these societies actually felt like, from people on every side of it.

## Anchor questions

Sample prompts to open experiential work in this theme. Adapt to your room, and expect strong feeling. See the [[04-facilitator-guide|facilitator guide]] for general guidance on experiential prompts.

- What were you taught about "communist countries," by whom, and in what setting?
- If you have lived in or visited one of these states, what surprised you?
- Does anyone in your family carry a story of these societies, of gratitude, of loss, or both?
- Have you relied on something one of these states is known for, free healthcare, education, housing? What would its absence mean?
- Have you feared or fled a state that called itself socialist? What did it do?
- What do you want to know that the usual stories never told you?

## Materials that may help

Experiential work begins from the room, and this theme's room often holds the history directly. These materials extend that testimony, deliberately from opposing sides. All are free unless marked otherwise.

### Testimony from the Soviet world

- **Svetlana Alexievich** (b. 1948), _Secondhand Time_ (2013). Assembled testimony of ordinary people living through the Soviet collapse, holding pride, loss, terror, and longing together. The single best balanced source for the lens. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/secondhandtimela0000alek)
- **Nadezhda Mandelstam** (1899 to 1980), _Hope Against Hope_ (1970). The poet's widow on his arrest and death in the Great Purge. The necessary weight on the critical side. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/hopeagainsthopem00mand)
- **Reimagining Soviet Georgia** (2020 to present). Oral histories of daily life and post-socialism in the South Caucasus, holding gains and losses together. Free: [podcasts.apple.com](https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/reimagining-soviet-georgia/id1555761205)
- **Working Class History** (2018 to present). First-hand testimony from ordinary people, including inside state-socialist societies. Free: [workingclasshistory.com](https://workingclasshistory.com/podcasts/)

### Inside the Asian and Eastern socialist states

- **A State of Mind** (Daniel Gordon, 2004, 93 min). Two young North Korean gymnasts and their families, observational and non-judgmental. A rare look at ordinary DPRK life. Free with ads: [watch.plex.tv](https://watch.plex.tv/movie/a-state-of-mind)
- **"North Koreans tell BBC they are stuck and waiting to die"** (BBC, 2023). Roughly 22 minutes, about 6.7 million views. Rare recent testimony on hunger and fear, the mainstream-critical counterweight to the film above. [youtube.com/watch?v=SiviOdWDl9o](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SiviOdWDl9o)
- **Sea of Memory** (2011, 25 min). The testimony of the Vietnamese boat people who fled after 1975, the voices of those the revolution drove out. [youtube.com/watch?v=YNe897ereaY](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YNe897ereaY)
- **The Death of Yugoslavia** (BBC, 1995, 287 min). The protagonists narrate the collapse of Yugoslav socialism into war, first-person and unsparing. [youtube.com/watch?v=bVUg-VoPAeA](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bVUg-VoPAeA)

## A note on method

This is the theme where a facilitator most needs to protect the room. People arrive with inherited loyalties and inherited wounds, and a session can slide into a shouting match between "you're defending dictators" and "you're repeating cold-war propaganda." Name that risk out loud at the start. Insist that a literacy campaign and a labor camp can both be true, that an exile's grief and a worker's gratitude are both knowledge, and that the goal is to understand what happened, not to win. Testimony from every side has standing here.
