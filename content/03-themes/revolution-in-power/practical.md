_What is to be done? How do we act on what we have learned, and how do people already act?_

See [[02-pedagogy#Practical|the pedagogy document]] for what this lens does across all themes.

For most participants the practical question is not "how would I run a planned economy" but something closer to home: how should a socialist in an imperialist country relate to the states that claim the socialist name, past and present? With uncritical solidarity? With principled criticism? With a blanket refusal? China's rise, Vietnam's reforms, the long shadow of the Indonesian massacres, and the wreckage of the 1989 to 1991 collapse are not settled history; they are argued over in real time, and how the left reads them shapes what it does. This lens gathers those live arguments and asks the room to locate itself in them honestly, rather than adopt a slogan. It ties to the solidarity questions in [[03-themes/imperialism-and-the-global-system/index|Imperialism and the Global System]].

## Anchor questions

- Is China today a socialist project, a capitalist one, or a contradiction still in motion? What follows for us if we decide?
- Vietnam and China both went to market and grew fast. Was that a betrayal, a survival strategy, or a road worth studying?
- How should socialists here relate to these states, with solidarity, criticism, or both at once, and how do you do both without collapsing into one?
- Is "campism," backing any state the US opposes, solidarity or a substitute for it?
- What does an honest reckoning with the crimes, the 1965 massacres, the purges, the camps, actually require of us?
- Who paid for the transition out of socialism, and what does that teach about how it should never be done?

## Materials

All free unless marked otherwise. The set is built to argue with itself; each source's stance is named.

### The China and Vietnam reform debate

- **Monthly Review, China archive.** An ongoing sympathetic-Marxist argument over whether China is building socialism or restoring capitalism. Free: [monthlyreview.org](https://monthlyreview.org/subject/china/)

### The lessons of the collapse

- **The Shock Doctrine** (Winterbottom and Klein, 2009, 79 min). How the post-Soviet collapse was used to force through privatization, and who profited. [youtube.com/watch?v=B3B5qt6gsxY](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B3B5qt6gsxY)
- **Stuckler, King, and McKee**, "Mass privatisation and the post-communist mortality crisis" (_The Lancet_, 2009). Rapid privatization linked to a surge in deaths, with the data. Free PDF: [mltoday.com](https://mltoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/lancet-privatisation.pdf)
- **Kristen Ghodsee** and **Mitchell A. Orenstein**, _Taking Stock of Shock_ (2021). Puts the economic data, the survey data, and the ethnography of the transition side by side and finds they disagree: by the aggregates 1989 was a success, and by the lives it was not, with the cost falling on women, the old, and the east. The feminist account of who paid, and the answer to the Shock Doctrine question of what happened to the people afterward. Paywalled: [global.oup.com](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/taking-stock-of-shock-9780197549247). The authors' free companion site carries an introduction PDF, the data, and their talks: [takingstockofshock.com](https://www.takingstockofshock.com/)

## A note on method

The trap in this lens is that "what is to be done" becomes "which side are you on," a loyalty test rather than a practice. Resist it. A useful session ends not with the room agreeing on a verdict about China but with each participant able to state the strongest version of a position they do not hold. Solidarity that cannot criticize and criticism that offers no solidarity are both cheap; the practical skill worth building here is holding the two together under pressure.
