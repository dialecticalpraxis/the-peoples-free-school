_What would freedom actually require here? What are people already doing to build it, and what remains unresolved?_

See [[02-pedagogy#Liberatory|the pedagogy document]] for what this lens does across all themes.

This is the lens where the theme's central argument stays open, because the school does not hold a verdict on it and does not think the room should be handed one. Did these revolutions expand human freedom or crush it? Both, unevenly, and the accounts collected here will not agree. What they share is a refusal to look away, either from the literacy campaigns, the clinics, and the defeat of fascism, or from the camps, the purges, and the silencing of the revolution's own left. The liberatory question is not "were they good or bad" but "what, in this record, is worth carrying forward, and what must never be repeated?" That is a question the room has to work out for itself. It connects to the questions of freedom in [[03-themes/anarchism-mutual-aid-and-prefiguration/index|Anarchism, Mutual Aid, and Prefiguration]] and to the reform-versus-revolution argument at the movement's source in [[03-themes/origins-dialectics-marx-and-the-workers-movement/index|Origins]].

## Anchor questions

- What in this experience genuinely expanded human freedom? What crushed it?
- Was Stalinism the fulfillment of the revolution, a betrayal of it, or something latent in it from the start?
- Kronstadt, the Cultural Revolution, 1956, 1968: how do we hold both the hope and the harm?
- Was Yugoslav self-management a real road beyond the command economy, or the same relations in a new costume?
- Can you have socialism without democracy? Did any of these societies have either?
- What would you refuse to give up, and what would you refuse to repeat?

## Debates and disagreements

The whole theme is a debate, and this lens keeps it open rather than closing it. Some threads to hold, not resolve:

- **Was it socialism at all?** Trotsky says degenerated workers' state; Cliff and Wolff say state capitalism; Djilas says a new class society; Dunayevskaya asks whether the word applies where workers never ruled; Parenti and Martens say yes, socialism under siege. The room will not settle this, and should not pretend to.
- **The Stalin question.** Losurdo and Martens against Mandelstam, Luxemburg, and the anarchists. A facilitator should make sure the defenders' texts are read _and_ that the victims' testimony from the [[03-themes/revolution-in-power/experiential|experiential lens]] is in the room when they are.
- **Was there another road?** The Yugoslav self-management experiment is the strongest candidate for a different model; Katalenac and Wainer disagree on whether it was one. Hold the question open.
- **What is worth carrying forward?** Free healthcare and education, mass literacy, the defeat of fascism sit on one side of the ledger; the camps, the famines, the surveillance, and the crushing of the revolution's own left sit on the other. Carrying the theme forward means being honest about both columns at once.
