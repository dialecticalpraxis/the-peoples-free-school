_How did this come to be? What were the conditions, struggles, and contingencies that produced the current arrangement?_

See [[02-pedagogy#Historical|the pedagogy document]] for what this lens does across all themes.

Anarchism is usually caricatured as chaos and bomb-throwing. Its actual history is a history of construction: millions of people running farms, factories, schools, and whole regions without bosses or a state. This lens traces that history, from Proudhon naming the tradition, through the Spanish collectives of 1936 and the Makhnovist free soviets, to the living experiments of the Zapatistas and Rojava. These are not utopias on paper. People built them, defended them, and in most cases had them crushed, and the record of how they worked is the strongest argument the tradition has.

## Anchor questions

- Anarchism is often caricatured. What did anarchists actually believe and do?
- In Spain in 1936, millions ran farms and factories without bosses. How, and what happened?
- What did the Zapatistas build in Chiapas, and how has it lasted thirty years?
- Why do the histories we inherit leave the cooperative and anarchist experiments out?
- When have ordinary people governed themselves, and what did it look like?

## Materials

Free unless marked otherwise. "Free to borrow" means a free Internet Archive account and a renewable one-hour loan.

### Books

- **Pierre-Joseph Proudhon** (1809 to 1865), _What is Property?_ (1840). The book that made "anarchist" a self-chosen label, arguing "property is robbery" and sketching a society of free contract and mutualist association rather than command. Free: [theanarchistlibrary.org](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/pierre-joseph-proudhon-what-is-property-an-inquiry-into-the-principle-of-right-and-of-government)
- **Sam Dolgoff** (1902 to 1990), _The Anarchist Collectives: Workers' Self-Management in the Spanish Revolution, 1936 to 1939_ (1974). Firsthand accounts of the CNT-FAI agrarian and industrial collectives, the fullest large-scale anarchist experiment in constructive, prefigurative economics. The anchor for this lens. Free: [theanarchistlibrary.org](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/sam-dolgoff-editor-the-anarchist-collectives)
- **Gaston Leval** (1895 to 1978), _Collectives in the Spanish Revolution_ (1975). A detailed eyewitness study of how villages, factories, and public services actually ran themselves under collectivization. Concrete ground for discussing what mutual aid looks like at scale. Free: [libcom.org](https://libcom.org/article/collectives-spanish-revolution-gaston-leval)
- **Peter Arshinov** (1887 to 1937), _History of the Makhnovist Movement (1918 to 1921)_ (1923). An insider history of the Ukrainian peasant anarchist insurgency around Nestor Makhno, its free soviets and land practices, and its crushing by the Bolshevik state. Free: [theanarchistlibrary.org](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/peter-arshinov-history-of-the-makhnovist-movement-1918-1921)
- **Emma Goldman** (1869 to 1940), _Living My Life_ (1931). Goldman's autobiography, the classic inside narrative of the US anarchist movement, its organizing, repression, and internal life from the 1880s onward. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/livingmylifeauto0000gold)

### Anarchism outside Europe

The experiments above are Spanish, Ukrainian, and Mexican. The tradition is wider than that, and the history has mostly been written as though it were not.

- **Alan MacSimoin**, _The Korean Anarchist Movement_ (1991). The Shinmin autonomous region in Manchuria ran a federated zone of roughly two million people through village, district, and area councils from 1929 to 1931, until the Japanese army and Stalinists between them destroyed it. It is the second-largest anarchist experiment ever attempted and almost nobody has heard of it. Free: [theanarchistlibrary.org](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/alan-macsimoin-the-korean-anarchist-movement)
- **libcom.org**, _Shifu and the Possibilities for Chinese Anarchism_ (2005). Liu Shifu organized the Cock Crow Society in Guangzhou in 1912 and printed anarchist anthologies five thousand at a time. Anarchism shaped the early Chinese labour movement before the Communist Party eclipsed it and then wrote it out. Free: [libcom.org](https://libcom.org/library/shifu-possibilities-chinese-anarchism)
- **libcom.org**, _Kotoku Shusui: Founder of Modern Anarchism in Japan_ (2005). A journalist who turned anarchist reading Kropotkin in prison, wrote an early critique of imperialism, and was hanged in the 1911 High Treason Incident on evidence the state largely invented. Free: [libcom.org](https://libcom.org/library/k%C3%B5toku-sh%C3%BCsui-founder-modern-anarchism-japan)

For African stateless traditions and for Zomia, see the theoretical lens, where Mbah and Igariwey's _African Anarchism_ and Scott's _The Art of Not Being Governed_ make the argument that statelessness is something people chose and built rather than something they had not got round to leaving.

### The women of the Spanish Revolution

- **Martha A. Ackelsberg**, _Free Women of Spain_ (1991). Mujeres Libres had 20,000 members, ran its own literacy schools and its own newspapers, and was told by the movement around it that the woman question could wait until after the revolution. Ackelsberg built the book on interviews with the women who did not wait. Read it directly against Dolgoff and Leval above, who describe the same revolution with the women mostly offstage. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/freewomenofspain0000acke)

### Primary sources

- **Peter Kropotkin** (1842 to 1921), _The Commune of Paris_ (1880). A short anarchist reading of the 1871 Commune as a living, if incomplete, prefiguration of federated self-government, and of the lessons its defeat holds for later movements. Free: [theanarchistlibrary.org](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/petr-kropotkin-the-commune-of-paris)
- **EZLN (Zapatista Army of National Liberation)**, _First Declaration of the Lacandon Jungle_ (1994). The New Year's Day 1994 declaration that opened the Zapatista uprising in Chiapas, the founding document of the most influential recent experiment in indigenous autonomy and self-government. Free PDF: [schoolsforchiapas.org](https://schoolsforchiapas.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/1st-Declaration-of-the-Lacandona-Jungle.pdf)

## Status

This lens has a developed first pass for this theme. Resources continue to be added as the project grows. Contributions welcome, especially histories of anarchism in Latin America beyond the Zapatistas, in South Asia, and in the Middle East, and of everyday cooperative and communal experiments. The Korean, Chinese, and Japanese movements are now here but each rests on a single short account; longer histories would be worth more.
