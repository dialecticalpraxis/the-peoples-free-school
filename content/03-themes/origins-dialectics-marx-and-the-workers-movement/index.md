title: "Origins: Dialectics, Marx, and the Workers' Movement"

Where did socialism come from? Not as a finished blueprint but as a method and a movement, hammered out over a long nineteenth century of philosophy, revolution, and defeat. This theme is the first of a three-part series on enacted socialism, and it covers the beginning: Hegel's dialectic and Feuerbach's materialism; the young Marx forging historical materialism out of and against them; the revolutions of 1848 and the Communist Manifesto; the seventy-two days of the Paris Commune; the First and Second Internationals; the great mass parties and the argument that split them, reform or revolution. It gives the socialist tradition its foundations before the next themes follow it into power. The _economics_ Marx built lives in [[03-themes/how-capitalism-works/index|How Capitalism Works]] and the _theory of the state_ in [[03-themes/the-state-strategy-and-power/index|The State, Strategy, and Power]]; this theme is where the ideas and the movement are born.

## The series

This is **Part 1 of three** on the socialist tradition and its states, read best in order:

1. **Origins: Dialectics, Marx, and the Workers' Movement** (this theme), the idea and the movement before power.
2. [[03-themes/revolution-in-power/index|Revolution in Power: the USSR, China, and the Eastern Bloc]], the revolutions that seized the state and what became of them.
3. [[03-themes/socialism-and-national-liberation/index|Socialism and National Liberation: the Global South]], Cuba, the Sahel, Latin America, and the Caribbean.

## How to use this theme

This folder organizes resources by lens. See the [[02-pedagogy|Pedagogy document]] for what the lenses do and why they are structured this way.

- [[03-themes/origins-dialectics-marx-and-the-workers-movement/experiential|Experiential]]: What is your experience with this? What does the room already know?
- [[03-themes/origins-dialectics-marx-and-the-workers-movement/historical|Historical]]: How did this come to be?
- [[03-themes/origins-dialectics-marx-and-the-workers-movement/theoretical|Theoretical]]: How do we understand and analyze it?
- [[03-themes/origins-dialectics-marx-and-the-workers-movement/practical|Practical]]: What is happening on the ground and what are people doing about it?
- [[03-themes/origins-dialectics-marx-and-the-workers-movement/liberatory|Liberatory]]: What would it mean to overcome or transform this?

A session does not need to use all five lenses. Because the primary texts here are short, this theme rewards reading one aloud together, the _Theses on Feuerbach_, the opening of the Manifesto, a few pages of _Reform or Revolution_, and arguing it out.

## Anchor questions

These questions have proven useful for opening up this theme. They are starting points, not a script.

- What did you first hear about Marx or socialism, and who told you?
- What is a dialectic, and what does it mean to say history moves by contradiction?
- Marx said he turned Hegel right side up. What did he keep, and what did he invert?
- Why does a defeated experiment like the Paris Commune still matter so much to this tradition?
- Reform or revolution: is that still the real choice, or a false one?
- What did building this movement actually involve, and what can organizers still learn from it?

## Recommended sequences

Suggested arcs for groups new to this theme. Adapt freely. Every session opens with a check-in and closes with reflection (see [[02-pedagogy#Reflective practice|the pedagogy document]]).

**Single-session entry point (2 hours).** Open experiential: what people first heard about Marx and socialism, and the feeling attached. Watch a short piece together (["Political Theory: Karl Marx"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fSQgCy_iIcc), noting its liberal framing to argue with). Read the _Theses on Feuerbach_ aloud, all eleven, and dwell on the last. Close on: reform or revolution, and is that the real choice? See [[single-session|the single-session template]].

**Six-week study circle.** Week 1 experiential: our own inheritance about socialism, plus the Commune through Lissagaray or Watkins's film. Week 2 theoretical: Hegel and Feuerbach, the dialectic and its materialist turn. Week 3 theoretical: the young Marx, the 1844 Manuscripts, _Theses on Feuerbach_, _The German Ideology_. Week 4 historical: 1848, the Commune, the First International. Week 5 historical and practical: the mass parties, the Erfurt Program, and how the movement organized. Week 6 liberatory: reform versus revolution, Bernstein against Luxemburg, held open. The Marxists.org study guides and the Ithaca DSA syllabus can structure the reading. See [[study-circle-6week|the six-week template]].

**Weekend intensive.** Compress the arc into a Friday-through-Sunday structure: Saturday on the philosophy (dialectics and the young Marx), Sunday on the movement (1848, the Commune, the Internationals, reform versus revolution). A screening of _The Young Karl Marx_ on Friday evening or _La Commune_ on Saturday works well. See [[weekend-intensive|the weekend-intensive template]].
