_What would freedom actually require here? What are people already doing to build it, and what remains unresolved?_

See [[02-pedagogy#Liberatory|the pedagogy document]] for what this lens does across all themes.

The national-liberation revolutions carried some of the most powerful visions of freedom in the whole socialist tradition: Che's new human being, Sankara's self-reliant and ecological Burkina Faso, Bishop's small island answering to nobody, Rodney's grounding with the dispossessed. This lens holds those visions up in full, and it holds them honest. Because these revolutions also produced their own reckonings: the Sandinista government that turned on its people, the "indigenous state" that critics say was neither, the guerrilla strategy that failed again and again. The school takes no verdict. It asks what in these emancipatory projects is worth carrying forward and what must never be repeated, and it leaves the room to decide. It connects to the freedom questions in [[03-themes/anarchism-mutual-aid-and-prefiguration/index|Anarchism, Mutual Aid, and Prefiguration]] and the ecological horizon of [[03-themes/ecology-and-the-climate-crisis/index|Ecology and the Climate Crisis]].

## Anchor questions

- Che wanted to create a "new man." Is that inspiring, dangerous, or both?
- Sankara refused the debt and planted trees. What would a liberation that is also ecological look like now?
- "Every cook can govern." Did any of these revolutions actually put ordinary people in charge, or only new elites?
- Nicaragua's revolution turned authoritarian. Was that the revolution's fault, the siege's, or Ortega's? Does it discredit 1979?
- What in these projects would you carry forward, and what would you refuse to repeat?

- **Thomas Sankara**, _Imperialism Is the Arsonist of Our Forests and Savannas_ (1986). Liberation as ecological, decades early. Free: [marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/sankara/1986/february/05.htm)

### The revolution's own critics

The hardest voices to dismiss are not the enemies of these revolutions but the people who made them and then said what happened next. None of these four is an outside critic; every one of them is a participant.

- **Dora Maria Tellez** (b. 1955). Comandante Dos, who at 22 helped seize Nicaragua's National Palace and later served as health minister, on why she broke with Ortega. His government jailed her, held her in solitary for 605 days, and stripped her of citizenship. This is the revolution's own commander describing what became of it. Free: [thenation.com](https://www.thenation.com/article/world/dora-maria-tellez-interview/) and, on the solitary confinement and the statelessness, [havanatimes.org](https://havanatimes.org/interviews/dora-maria-tellez-is-back-after-605-days-in-solitary/)
- **Sergio Ramirez** (b. 1942). The Sandinista vice-president turned novelist, exiled and stripped of citizenship by the government he helped build. His memoir _Adios Muchachos_ is paywalled at [dukeupress.edu](https://www.dukeupress.edu/adios-muchachos) with no public borrowing copy; his free interview is at [confidencial.digital](https://confidencial.digital/en/english/sergio-ramirez-if-i-returned-to-nicaragua-i-would-go-straight-to-masatepe/) (this host blocks automated checks but is live)
- **Ernest Harsch**, _Thomas Sankara: A Reassessment_ (2013). Twelve pages that weigh the literacy drives, the vaccinations, and the land reform against the People's Revolutionary Courts, the press restrictions, and what the CDRs did. Sympathetic and unwilling to be hagiographic, which is exactly what the Sankara material here needed. Free: [cdr.lib.unc.edu](https://cdr.lib.unc.edu/concern/articles/mk61rv13k?locale=en)
- **On ujamaa's coerced villagization**: **Goran Hyden**, _No Shortcuts to Progress_ (1983), argues Tanzania's peasantry could not be administered into socialism and that forced resettlement failed on its own terms. Free to borrow: [archive.org](https://archive.org/details/noshortcutstopro0000hyde). **James C. Scott**'s _Seeing Like a State_ (1998) makes ujamaa its central case for why high-modernist schemes destroy the local knowledge that makes a place work. Paywalled: [yalebooks.yale.edu](https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300246759/seeing-like-a-state/). The free companion, written from the continent rather than about it, is [africanarguments.org](https://africanarguments.org/2020/12/tanzania-remembering-ujamaa-the-good-the-bad-and-the-buried/)

## Debates and disagreements

This lens keeps the theme's hardest questions open. Some threads to hold, not resolve:

- **The vision and the state.** Che, Sankara, and Bishop offered luminous visions of the new society; all three also built centralized states, and two were killed before the contradictions fully played out. Can these visions be separated from the vanguard forms that carried them?
- **Nicaragua, then and now.** Norton and Robinson disagree over whether today's Ortega government betrays 1979 or continues it. Read them together and let the room weigh whether a revolution is discredited by what its leaders later became.
- **The indigenous state.** Rivera Cusicanqui says Bolivia's MAS gave the state an indigenous face without an indigenous soul; García Linera, in the [[03-themes/socialism-and-national-liberation/theoretical|theoretical lens]], says the tensions were creative and unavoidable. Whose account fits the record?
- **Did foco work?** The romance of the guerrilla is strong; the body count of failed foco insurgencies is real. What is worth keeping from Che's strategy, and what got people killed?

## Status

This lens has a developed first pass for this theme. Resources continue to be added as the project grows. Tellez and Ramirez are now here in their own voices. Contributions welcome, especially liberatory currents from the Portuguese-African revolutions, and Burkinabe voices on Sankara: the reassessment here is written by an American, and the critique of a revolution is worth most from the people who lived under it.
